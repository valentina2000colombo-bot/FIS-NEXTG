NEXT-G
How new genes emerge: exploring a model of novel gene creation following the evolution of wheat

★ Funded by the Italian Fund for Science

This project studies how certain mobile DNA elements (transposons), often dismissed as "junk DNA", can turn into fully-fledged new genes. Using wheat, one of the world's most important crops, with a well-known evolutionary history, the research will reconstruct when and how these new genes emerged, aiming to identify sequences that could help improve the resilience of future crops.

The project

Abstract

Transposable elements (TEs) are DNA sequences able to move within the genome. Their activity is normally kept in check by the host organism, but under certain conditions part of these sequences can be "repurposed" (exapted) into a fully functional gene with a useful role for the plant. This phenomenon, known as exaptation, is still poorly studied and understood.

NEXT-G will use wheat — a crop whose evolutionary history, including the hybridisation and polyploidisation events that shaped its genome, is very well characterised — as the ideal system to identify these transposon-derived genes and reconstruct when and how they arose, combining bioinformatics, functional genomics and metagenomic approaches.

The project will provide the first systematic map of these genes in wheat, helping to understand how new genes form and how this may have historically contributed to the domestication and adaptation of wheat to diverse environments — with potential applications for breeding more resilient crops in the face of climate change.

Research structure

Project objectives

The project is organised into 3 scientific objectives (P1-P3), alongside the cross-cutting Coordination (P0) and Dissemination and data management (P4) activities.

Objective 01

Identify transposon-derived genes in wheat

Build the first extensive collection of exapted transposable elements (ETEs) in the bread wheat genome, using a bioinformatic pipeline based on HMM models already validated in other species.

Objective 02

Characterise their biological role

Analyse tissue-specific expression and DNA methylation patterns of these genes to understand what functions they perform and how they are regulated across the plant's developmental stages.

Objective 03

Study their role in wheat adaptation

Extend the analysis to 50 cultivars and 50 landraces of wheat, to assess whether these genes contributed to adaptation to different environments during domestication.

How we work

Methodology and timeline

The project runs for 36 months and is organised into three main phases (pillars), each with its own timeframe, alongside ongoing coordination and dissemination activities.

Pillar 1 — Months 1-24

Identification of ETEs in the wheat genome

Collection of plant material, sequencing, and development of the bioinformatic pipeline to identify exapted transposable elements in bread wheat and its diploid and tetraploid relatives.

Pillar 2 — Months 8-36

Functional characterisation of ETEs

Analysis of tissue-specific expression and DNA methylation profiles to understand the biological role of the genes identified.

Pillar 3 — Months 8-36

Genomic variability across cultivars and landraces

Extension of the analysis to 50 cultivars and 50 landraces of wheat to assess the contribution of these genes to species adaptation.
